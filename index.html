Uneducated opinions and finger pointing

Documentation is for layoffs

August 8th, 2020

It's frustrating being onboarded to an existing project with literally zero documentation. Tiny scraps of an out of date wiki, comments that lie at the best of times, and hundreds of abandoned google docs with no semblance of organization or ownership. "We don't have time for documentation" is usually the answer, and often the sign of a company who doesn't manage it's time or it's projects well (at least in my experience). Worse still is the stigma that being asked to write documentation is one of the final stages leading up to a layoff (so that a replacement worker/team/offshore might get up to speed faster). But like any developer paradigm, writing and maintaining documentation is often done wrong, or not embraced for it's true value. Documentation forces us to confront and improve our design, to describe how something should and shouldn't work, and can effectively act as a fire escape during live operations crises. It is said that French kitchens use copper cookware that constantly needs polishing, a laborious and time consuming process. They could easily get away with stainless or nonstick, but when you step into that kitchen and see the polished copper cookware - it is a symbol of everything else that is being done right, and an acceptance of the time it takes to do things properly. I posit that a well documented product and company is the polished copper cookware of the software development industry.
