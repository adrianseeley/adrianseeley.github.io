Design by NotImplementedException

August 9th, 2020

Amidst the many paradigms to approach programming by, I find my process for rapid prototyping always remains agnostically the same: Given a task to be accomplished and no obvious design pattern, I start from the top down, filling in any operational pieces with NotImplementedException(s), or the language equivalent. For example, when making a game: a Game class, which holds a number of Screen classes, which need Transition(otherScreen) functions that are then filled with only NotImplementedException(s). The result is a hollow structure filled with lightweight yet meaningful method signatures, and no implementations. Thus allowing the designer to ignore how those screens might transition, and focus on the fact that they will need to at some point. Considering only the structure enables a dexterous approach to moving cumbersome pieces of code around early on without worrying about the side effects of their implementation. Often by the end of this process (when done correctly), a team of junior developers is able to sweep through writing unit tests and filling in code stubs to quickly reach a working prototype. Often this process of populating the implementation details of the code reveals structural issues and oversights, which then leads to partial refactoring or in some cases another prototype. Worry about the forest first, and the trees should follow.

Documentation is for layoffs

August 8th, 2020

It's frustrating being onboarded to an existing project with zero documentation. Tiny scraps of an out of date wiki, comments that lie at the best of times, and troves of abandoned google docs with no semblance of organization or ownership. "We don't have time for documentation" is an all too common answer, and often the sign of a company not managing it's time or it's projects well. Worse still is the stigma that being asked to write documentation is one of the final stages leading up to a layoff - so that a replacement worker/team/offshore might get up to speed faster. Following the fashion of most developer paradigms, writing and maintaining documentation is often done incorrectly, or not embraced for it's true value. Documentation should force us to confront and improve our design, to describe how something should and shouldn't work, and effectively act as a fire escape during a live operations failure. French kitchens use copper cookware that constantly needs polishing (a laborious and time consuming process), when they could easily get away with stainless or nonstick. But when you step into that kitchen and see the polished copper cookware - it is a symbol of everything else that is being done right, and an acceptance of the time it takes to do things properly. I posit that a well documented product and company is the polished copper cookware of the software development industry.
